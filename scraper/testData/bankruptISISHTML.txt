                        	<h1 itemprop="headline" id="story-heading" class="story-heading">What It Will Take to Bankrupt ISIS </h1>
                        <div id="story-meta-footer" class="story-meta-footer">
                                                                                    

<p class="byline-dateline"><span class="byline" itemprop="author creator" itemscope itemtype="http://schema.org/Person"  itemid="http://www.nytimes.com/interactive/opinion/editorialboard.html">By <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/interactive/opinion/editorialboard.html" rel="author" title="More Articles by THE EDITORIAL BOARD"><span class="byline-author" data-byline-name="THE EDITORIAL BOARD" itemprop="name">THE EDITORIAL BOARD</span></a></span><time class="dateline" datetime="2015-12-03" itemprop="datePublished" content="2015-12-03">DEC. 3, 2015</time>

        <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="396" data-total-count="396" itemprop="articleBody" id="story-continues-1">Even before the Paris attacks, the United States and its allies escalated <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2015/11/13/us/politics/us-steps-up-its-attacks-on-isis-controlled-oil-fields-in-syria.html">airstrikes</a> against the Islamic State’s oil facilities in eastern Syria. Hundreds of tanker trucks, carting oil to markets in Turkey and Iraq, have also been bombed as part of a plan to cripple production. Apart from this effort, the challenge now is attacking the group’s sources of revenue that extend far beyond oil.</p><aside id='middle-right-paid-post-container' class='ad middle-right-ad paid-post-ad hidden nocontent robots-nocontent'>
                                <h2 class='marginalia-heading'></h2>
                                    <ul class='story-menu'>
                                        <li id='MiddleRightPaidPost1' class='story-menu-item ad'></li>
                                        <li id='MiddleRightPaidPost2' class='story-menu-item ad'></li>
                                        <li id='MiddleRightPaidPost3' class='story-menu-item ad'></li>
                                        <li id='MiddleRightPaidPost4' class='story-menu-item ad'></li>
                                    </ul>
                                </aside><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="405" data-total-count="801" itemprop="articleBody" id="story-continues-2">The Islamic State, also known as ISIS or ISIL, may be the best-financed terrorist franchise in history. In the territory it controls in Iraq and Syria, it extorts hundreds of millions of dollars a year in “taxes,” tolls, and profits from businesses and residents, as <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2015/11/30/world/middleeast/predatory-islamic-state-wrings-money-from-those-it-rules.html">reported</a><a title="Predatory Islamic State Wrings Money From Those It Rules" href="http://www.nytimes.com/2015/11/30/world/middleeast/predatory-islamic-state-wrings-money-from-those-it-rules.html"> by The Times</a>. It also earns millions by looting banks, through <a title="Paying Ransoms, Europe Bankrolls Qaeda Terror" href="http://www.nytimes.com/2014/07/30/world/africa/ransoming-citizens-europe-becomes-al-qaedas-patron.html">ransoms</a> for kidnap victims, and selling plundered antiquities.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="358" data-total-count="1159" itemprop="articleBody">All of this makes it harder to close off the money spigot, but the United States and its allies can claim some success. For example, this summer the Americans finally persuaded the <a title="How Iraq Subsidizes Islamic State" href="http://www.nytimes.com/2015/02/05/opinion/how-iraq-subsidizes-islamic-state.html">Iraqi government</a> to stop paying salaries to public employees in at least one ISIS-controlled province, which meant the group could no longer extort huge sums from those workers.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="435" data-total-count="1594" itemprop="articleBody">Oil is still a major income source, bringing in an estimated $40 million a month before the latest airstrikes. <a title="Warplanes Blast Militants’ Refineries in Syria, Targeting a Source of Cash" href="http://www.nytimes.com/2014/09/26/world/middleeast/isis-revenue-sources-remain-crucial-target-us-says.html">Oil fields and facilities</a> were an early target when the American-led campaign began in August 2014. Washington, however, pulled back as it tried to balance fighting the Islamic State and maintaining energy supplies for civilians. That measured approach may no longer make sense, given the widening threat posed by the group.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="468" data-total-count="2062" itemprop="articleBody">After the earlier strikes, the Islamic State’s engineers were able to quickly repair damaged facilities. The current bombing is intended to make sure oil production facilities are out of commission for at least six months. Fearing civilian casualties, the Obama administration initially balked at bombing the tanker trucks, operated by middlemen, that are the main oil distribution network. Now, the Pentagon is <a title="U.S. Warplanes Strike ISIS Oil Trucks in Syria" href="http://www.nytimes.com/2015/11/17/world/middleeast/us-strikes-syria-oil.html">dropping leaflets</a> before an airstrike to warn drivers.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="206" data-total-count="2268" itemprop="articleBody">The Treasury Department has also moved against oil sales. Last week it put sanctions on <a title="U.S. Announces Sanctions on 2 Accused of Dealings With Assad or ISIS" href="http://www.nytimes.com/2015/11/26/us/politics/us-announces-sanctions-on-2-accused-of-dealings-with-assad-or-isis.html">George Haswani</a>, a Syrian businessman who acts as a middleman for the Islamic State’s oil sales to the Syrian regime.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="723" data-total-count="2991" itemprop="articleBody" id="story-continues-3">The United States and its partners should also intensify efforts to cut off the group’s access to the international financial system. The <a href="http://www.fatf-gafi.org/media/fatf/documents/reports/Financing-of-the-terrorist-organisation-ISIL.pdf">Financial Action Task Force</a>, an international body that combats the financing of terrorism, said in a February report that there were more than 20 Syrian financial institutions operating in ISIS-held parts of Syria. While most major banks have probably severed ties with these institutions, the report said the Syrian banks were still interacting with some unidentified “jurisdictions,” which experts say include Russia, Lebanon and Turkey. Russia and Iran, the main allies of President <a title="Read more stories about Bashar al-Assad" href="http://www.nytimes.com/topic/person/bashar-alassad?inline=nyt-per">Bashar al-Assad</a> of Syria, should use their leverage to persuade him to close these channels.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="426" data-total-count="3417" itemprop="articleBody" id="story-continues-4">The Islamic State relies on foreign donors far less than Al Qaeda, but it has been inventive, raising money by crowdsourcing and having its members take out bank loans. The network of international financiers is far-flung. In September, the Treasury Department imposed <a href="http://www.usatoday.com/story/news/politics/2015/09/29/us-sanctions-islamic-state-isis-isil/73023358/">sanctions</a> on more than 30 individuals and entities from 11 countries — including Saudi Arabia, Britain and Indonesia — for bankrolling the Islamic State.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="362" data-total-count="3779" itemprop="articleBody">In recent days, the United Nations <a href="http://www.un.org/press/en/2015/sc12132.doc.htm">Security Council</a> and the <a href="http://www.diplomatie.gouv.fr/en/french-foreign-policy/defence-security/terrorism/events/article/g20-statement-on-the-fight-against-terrorism-11-17-15">Group of 20</a> have reiterated promises to crack down on the financing of terrorists. The Treasury Department says it has provided some 500 leads to European counterterrorism authorities. It won’t be easy to empty the Islamic State’s coffers, but the Paris attacks should spur the world to try harder.</p>
